Attendance Requirement and Ensuring the Right to Education

Senan was studying at a higher education institution. During the semester, due to a serious illness, he was unable to attend classes for a long period. Nevertheless, he did not completely fall behind in his studies, learned the materials independently, and prepared for exams.

At the end of the semester, however, the university informed him that according to the existing rules, he could not be admitted to exams due to insufficient attendance. The rules required students to attend a certain portion of classes, and exceeding this threshold βÄî namely missing more than 25 percent of class hours βÄî was considered grounds for exclusion from exams. This rule was applied without exception, and SenanβÄôs illness was not taken into account.

The issue therefore arose as to whether the application of such attendance rules without considering valid reasons leads to a restriction of the studentβÄôs right to education. The Commissioner for Human Rights (Ombudsman) of the Republic of Azerbaijan applied to the Constitutional Court for a legal assessment of this matter.

The Constitutional Court noted that the right to education is a fundamental right guaranteed by the Constitution, and the state has the authority to establish certain rules in this field. However, these rules must be applied in a fair and proportional manner. Any restriction must not eliminate the essence of the right.

The Court emphasized that the automatic application of attendance requirements without considering valid reasons, including health conditions, may lead to a disproportionate restriction of the right to education. Therefore, individual circumstances must be assessed in each case.

Ultimately, this approach demonstrated that legal norms should be applied not only formally, but also in line with the essence of human rights. Ensuring the right to education requires taking into account the specific situation of each individual.
